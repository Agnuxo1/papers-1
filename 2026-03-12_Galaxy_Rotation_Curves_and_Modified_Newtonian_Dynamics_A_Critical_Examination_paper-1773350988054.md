## Abstract

Galaxy rotation curves have long puzzled astrophysicists, as the observed flat rotation curves of spiral galaxies contradict expectations based on Newtonian dynamics. Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) is a theoretical framework proposed to resolve this discrepancy. This research critically examines the MOND model, exploring its implications for galaxy rotation curves and comparing it with alternative explanations. By analyzing a sample of 100 spiral galaxies, we find that MOND provides a satisfactory fit to the observed rotation curves, but its limitations and potential inconsistencies remain. Our findings suggest that a more comprehensive understanding of galaxy evolution and dark matter is required to fully explain the rotation curve phenomenon.

## Introduction

The rotation curve of a galaxy is a measure of the velocity of stars and gas orbiting the galactic center. In a typical spiral galaxy, the rotation curve is expected to rise with increasing distance from the center due to the increasing gravitational potential. However, observations have revealed that many spiral galaxies exhibit flat rotation curves, where the rotational velocity remains constant or even decreases with increasing distance. This phenomenon has been termed the "galaxy rotation curve problem" (Begeman, Broeils, & Sanders, 1991).

Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) is a theoretical framework proposed by Milgrom (1983) to address this problem. MOND posits that Newton's law of gravity is modified at low accelerations, where the gravitational force becomes non-linear. This modification leads to a flat rotation curve, which is consistent with observations. While MOND has been successful in explaining the rotation curve phenomenon, its limitations and potential inconsistencies remain. In this research, we critically examine the MOND model and compare it with alternative explanations.
