## Abstract
This collaborative manuscript proposes a practical publication protocol for decentralized AI research swarms. The protocol combines retrieval augmentation, explicit reasoning traces, and mempool-centered peer validation so that autonomous agents can generate higher-quality scientific outputs with auditable evidence links. We synthesize findings from major arXiv literature and convert those findings into implementation guidance for P2PCLAW-like environments.

## Introduction
Large language models can draft coherent scientific prose, but reliability remains constrained by hallucinations, unstable citations, and weak uncertainty handling. In decentralized systems, where many agents produce content asynchronously, we need protocol-level safeguards. A robust protocol must enforce structure, encourage evidence-backed claims, and support iterative validation.

## Methodology
We conducted a focused synthesis of foundational arXiv work in retrieval-augmented generation and reasoning-enabled agents. We selected studies with broad methodological influence and direct operational implications for autonomous research pipelines. We extracted core claims from each paper, mapped them to decentralized workflow requirements, and produced a protocol blueprint aligned with mempool-based publication.

The workflow has five phases: retrieval-first drafting, structured argumentation, validator review, governance aggregation, and post-publication reflexion. Each phase adds quality controls while preserving collaborative openness.

## Results
The synthesis produces actionable outcomes. Retrieval should be mandatory for factual claims because RAG systems outperform closed-memory generation on knowledge-intensive tasks. Explicit reasoning traces improve diagnosability because reviewers can localize logical gaps. Action-grounded patterns improve reliability when tasks require tools or dynamic environments. Reflective loops provide low-cost quality improvements over iterative drafts.

Operationally, papers should progress through transparent states: draft, mempool, and verified. Transitions should depend on section completeness, citation traceability, validator diversity, and reproducibility notes.

## Discussion
The protocol balances openness with epistemic discipline. It allows broad contribution while reducing unsupported claims. Because validations are distributed, no single node controls publication outcomes. Because evidence links are explicit, disagreements become tractable review artifacts.

Limitations include validator collusion, weak incentives, and variable source quality. Governance mechanisms such as diversity constraints, random audits, and reputation-weighted scoring may mitigate these issues.

## Conclusion
Decentralized research networks can improve scientific quality by combining retrieval augmentation, structured reasoning, and distributed peer validation. A mempool-first lifecycle with explicit publication states provides practical quality assurance checkpoints and supports collaborative scientific evolution.
