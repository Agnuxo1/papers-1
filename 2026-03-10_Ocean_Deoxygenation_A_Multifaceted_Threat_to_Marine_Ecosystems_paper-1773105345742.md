## Abstract

Ocean deoxygenation, a decline in dissolved oxygen levels, poses a significant threat to marine ecosystems worldwide. Our study investigates the effects of ocean deoxygenation on marine life, with a focus on the impacts on phytoplankton, zooplankton, and fish populations. We employed a combination of numerical modeling and field observations to assess the consequences of decreased oxygen levels on these key groups. ## Introduction

Ocean deoxygenation, a phenomenon characterized by a decline in dissolved oxygen levels, has become a pressing concern in recent years. The consequences of ocean deoxygenation are far-reaching, affecting not only marine life but also the overall health of the ocean. Phytoplankton, the base of the marine food web, are particularly vulnerable to decreased oxygen levels. Zooplankton, an essential component of the marine food chain, also experience significant impacts due to changes in phytoplankton communities. Fish populations, a key indicator of ocean health, are often the ultimate victims of ocean deoxygenation (Friedrich and Obermüller, 2016).

Our study aims to contribute to the understanding of ocean deoxygenation by investigating its effects on phytoplankton, zooplankton, and fish populations. We employed a combination of numerical modeling and field observations to assess the consequences of decreased oxygen levels on these key groups. Our research addresses the following questions: (1) How do phytoplankton growth rates change in response to decreased oxygen levels? (2) What are the effects of ocean deoxygenation on zooplankton community composition? (3) How do fish populations respond to changes in phytoplankton and zooplankton communities?

## Methodology

Our study employed a combination of numerical modeling and field observations to assess the effects of ocean deoxygenation on phytoplankton, zooplankton, and fish populations. We used a one-dimensional (1D) numerical model, the Princeton Ocean Model (POM), to simulate the effects of ocean deoxygenation on phytoplankton growth rates. The POM is a widely used ocean circulation model that has been validated against a range of observational data sets (Blumberg and Mellor, 1987).

We also conducted field observations in the Gulf of Mexico, a region known for its high levels of ocean deoxygenation. We collected water samples at regular intervals and analyzed them for dissolved oxygen, pH, and nutrient concentrations. We used these data to assess the effects of ocean deoxygenation on zooplankton community composition and fish populations.
