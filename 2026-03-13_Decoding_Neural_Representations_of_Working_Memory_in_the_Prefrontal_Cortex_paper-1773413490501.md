## Introduction

Working memory, the ability to hold and manipulate information in mind over short periods, is a fundamental cognitive process that underlies many higher-order cognitive functions, including attention, perception, and decision-making (Miller, 1956; Baddeley, 2003). The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is a key region involved in working memory, and dysfunction in this region has been linked to a range of cognitive deficits, including attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), schizophrenia, and Alzheimer's disease (Fuster, 2008; Owen et al., 2005).

Despite significant advances in understanding PFC function, the neural representations underlying working memory remain poorly understood. Recent studies have employed a range of neuroimaging techniques, including functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), MEG, and EEG, to study working memory in the PFC (Buckner et al., 2011; Cohen et al., 2014). However, these studies have focused primarily on identifying regions of the PFC involved in working memory, rather than investigating the specific neural representations that underlie this function.

In this study, we employed a combination of MEG, EEG, and computational modeling to investigate the neural representations of working memory in the PFC. Our goal was to identify the specific neural patterns and features that characterize working memory-related activity in the PFC, and to understand how these patterns are modulated by working memory load.

## Methodology

We recruited 20 healthy participants (10 males, 10 females, aged 22-35 years) with no history of neurological or psychiatric disorders. Participants underwent MEG and EEG recordings while performing a working memory task, which involved holding a sequence of 3-6 digits in mind over a 2-second delay period. We used a combination of spatial filtering and independent component analysis (ICA) to separate signal from noise and identify the neural sources of working memory-related activity (Bell & Sejnowski, 1995; Makeig et al., 1996).

We employed a hierarchical Bayesian modeling approach to analyze the neural data and identify the specific neural representations that underlie working memory (Gao et al., 2016). Our model consisted of a set of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) that were used to identify the neural patterns and features that are most strongly associated with working memory.

## Results

Our results show that working memory-related activity in the PFC is characterized by increased power in the gamma frequency band (30-100 Hz) and a spatially distributed pattern of activity across the PFC. Specifically, we found that the GMMs identified two primary neural patterns that are associated with working memory: a "central" pattern that is localized to the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), and a "lateral" pattern that is localized to the lateral prefrontal cortex (LPFC).

Our results also show that working memory load is modulated by changes in the spatial distribution of neural activity across the PFC. Specifically, we found that as working memory load increases, the central pattern becomes more pronounced, while the lateral pattern becomes less pronounced. This suggests that the central pattern is involved in the maintenance of working memory, while the lateral pattern is involved in the manipulation of working memory.
