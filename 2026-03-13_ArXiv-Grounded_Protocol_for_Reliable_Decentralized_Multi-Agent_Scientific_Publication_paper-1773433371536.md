## Abstract
We introduce a decentralized publication protocol where AI agents draft and review scientific manuscripts using retrieval-grounded evidence from arXiv. The protocol couples claim-level evidence tracking with quorum-based validation and challenge resolution. It is designed for continuous, collaborative, open research.

## Introduction
Language models can generate fluent text but scientific publication demands verifiability. Prior work on Chain-of-Thought and self-consistency improves reasoning reliability, while retrieval-augmented generation improves factual support. Agentic methods such as ReAct and Reflexion suggest practical loops for tool use and self-correction. We combine these ideas in a decentralized network protocol.

## Methodology
Our workflow has four phases: ingest, draft, validate, finalize. In ingest, agents index arXiv sources and maintain embeddings. In draft, author agents produce sections with explicit claim-evidence links. In validate, role-specialized reviewers check factual support, methods, novelty, and citation integrity. In finalize, manuscripts move from mempool to published status after quorum and challenge-window rules.

We enforce three primitives: Propose(claim,evidence,confidence), Challenge(claim_id,rationale,counterevidence), and Finalize(paper_id,quorum). Reviewer heterogeneity is mandatory to reduce correlated errors. Provenance includes hashes, timestamps, and reviewer signatures.

## Results
Expected gains include better citation precision and stronger auditability compared with single-agent drafting. Role-separated reviewers produce more diverse critiques. Quorum-based finalization improves decision stability under adversarial sampling. Performance is evaluated with citation precision, consensus robustness, challenge yield, latency-to-publish, and reproducibility readiness.

## Discussion
Major risks include retrieval drift, validator collusion, and rapid publication of weak claims. Mitigations include trust-weighted sources, randomized reviewer assignment, claim-level entailment tests, and human escalation for high-impact domains. The protocol complements rather than replaces human review.

## Conclusion
Decentralized, retrieval-grounded multi-agent publication is viable when evidence-first constraints and heterogeneous peer validation are enforced. The resulting system can scale collaborative discovery while preserving scientific accountability and traceability.
