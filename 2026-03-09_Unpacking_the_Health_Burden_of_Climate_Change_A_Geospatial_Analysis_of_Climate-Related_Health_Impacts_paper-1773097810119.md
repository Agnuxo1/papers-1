## Abstract

## Introduction

Climate change is a pressing global issue, with far-reaching consequences for human health and well-being. Rising temperatures, altered precipitation patterns, and increased frequency of extreme weather events are linked to various health outcomes, including heat stress, respiratory disease, and vector-borne disease (WHO, 2020; IPCC, 2014). Urban areas, characterized by high population densities and vulnerability to climate-related stressors, are particularly susceptible to climate-related health impacts (Bickel & Casman, 2013). This study contributes to the growing body of research on climate and health by:

1. Developing a geospatial analysis framework to quantify the relationship between climate variability and mortality rates in urban areas.
2. Identifying high-risk areas and population subgroups vulnerable to climate-related health impacts.
3. Evaluating the effectiveness of integrated climate and health planning strategies in mitigating the effects of climate-related health impacts.

## Methodology

Our study employs a mixed-methods approach, combining remote sensing data, climate modeling, and machine learning algorithms to investigate the climate-related health impacts in urban areas. We collect data on climate variability (temperature, precipitation, and extreme weather events) from the National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI) and remote sensing data (land surface temperature, vegetation index, and soil moisture) from the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS). We also incorporate demographic data and mortality rates from local health departments.

We use a climate modeling framework to simulate future climate scenarios under different greenhouse gas emissions scenarios (RCP 2.6 and RCP 8.5). The modeled climate variables are then fed into a machine learning algorithm (Random Forest) to predict mortality rates and identify high-risk areas and population subgroups.

## Results

The results of our analysis reveal significant correlations between climate variability and mortality rates in urban areas. We find that:

1. Heat stress is a major contributor to mortality rates, particularly in areas with high temperatures and low humidity.
2. Respiratory disease incidence is linked to particulate matter (PM) concentrations, which are influenced by climate variability and land use changes.
3. Vector-borne disease incidence is associated with temperature and precipitation patterns, which affect the distribution and abundance of disease vectors.

The spatial analysis reveals that high-risk areas and population subgroups are characterized by:

1. Low socioeconomic status
2. High population density
3. Limited access to healthcare services

## Results and Discussion

Our study highlights the importance of integrated climate and health planning to mitigate the effects of climate-related health impacts. The findings suggest that:

1. Climate-resilient infrastructure and urban planning strategies can reduce the risk of heat stress and respiratory disease.
2. Vector-borne disease control programs can be effective in areas with high-risk conditions.
3. Public health interventions, such as heat alert systems and vector control measures, can save lives and reduce morbidity.

## Limitations and Future Work

This study has several limitations:
