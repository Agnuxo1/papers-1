## Abstract (150-300 words)

The convergence of quantum computing, multi-agent artificial intelligence, and climate science presents unprecedented opportunities for addressing complex environmental challenges. This paper proposes a novel framework integrating quantum machine learning techniques with decentralized multi-agent systems to advance climate modeling capabilities. Drawing on recent developments in quantum neural networks for cloud parameterization, log-mediated multi-agent reasoning architectures, and evolutionary trust mechanisms for decentralized agent economies, we present a comprehensive approach to climate simulation that leverages quantum advantages while maintaining robustness through decentralized coordination. Our framework addresses critical limitations in current climate models, including computational constraints, parameterization uncertainties, and the need for scalable, interpretable modeling approaches. We analyze the theoretical foundations of this integration, examine practical implementation considerations, and discuss implications for democratizing climate modeling capabilities across global research communities.

## Introduction

Climate change represents one of the most pressing challenges facing humanity in the twenty-first century. Accurate climate modeling is essential for understanding future climate scenarios, informing policy decisions, and guiding mitigation strategies. However, traditional climate models face significant computational limitations, particularly in representing cloud processes, turbulence, and other subgrid-scale phenomena that critically influence climate sensitivity. The integration of artificial intelligence and quantum computing offers promising avenues for overcoming these limitations.

Recent advances in quantum machine learning have demonstrated the potential for quantum neural networks to achieve comparable or superior performance to classical neural networks in climate modeling applications. Schwabe et al. (2025) showed that quantum neural networks can predict cloud cover with performance similar to classical neural networks while learning more consistent relationships. This suggests that quantum advantages may be particularly valuable in climate modeling, where model consistency and physical plausibility are paramount concerns.

Simultaneously, the field of multi-agent artificial intelligence has matured considerably, with new frameworks enabling sophisticated coordination between specialized agents. The Dealog framework, developed by Chakraborty et al. (2026), demonstrates how decentralized multi-agent systems can achieve competitive performance across diverse question-answering tasks through log-mediated reasoning and peer verification. These architectural innovations offer valuable lessons for climate modeling, where different model components must be carefully coordinated.

Furthermore, the deployment of AI systems in climate science must consider issues of global equity and access. Mozaffari et al. (2026) highlight the significant infrastructure inequalities that persist in AI-driven climate science, with development of foundation models concentrated predominantly in the Global North. This asymmetry risks perpetuating and amplifying existing climate information disparities. Decentralized approaches to climate modeling may help address these inequities by enabling broader participation in climate research.

## Methodology

We propose a novel framework that integrates quantum machine learning, multi-agent coordination, and evolutionary trust mechanisms for advanced climate modeling. The framework consists of three interconnected layers: a quantum parameterization layer, a multi-agent coordination layer, and a trust and governance layer.

The quantum parameterization layer employs quantum neural networks to learn improved parameterizations for subgrid-scale processes in climate models. Building on the work of Schwabe et al. (2025), we use variational quantum circuits to represent complex relationships between large-scale state variables and small-scale process rates. This layer can be trained on high-resolution simulation data or observational records to produce parameterizations that are more accurate and physically consistent than traditional schemes.

The multi-agent coordination layer manages the integration of different model components through specialized agents. Following the Dealog architecture (Chakraborty et al., 2026), we employ agents specialized for different aspects of climate modeling: radiation agents, convection agents, cloud agents, ocean circulation agents, and verification agents. These agents communicate through a shared log that serves as persistent memory and enables collaborative error detection.

The trust and governance layer implements the Ev-Trust mechanism (Wang et al., 2025) to ensure robust collaboration between agents. This layer creates incentives for agents to provide accurate parameterizations and to participate in verification activities. By embedding interaction history and reputation into revenue functions, the mechanism makes trustworthy behavior evolutionarily stable.

Our quantum parameterization approach uses a hybrid quantum-classical architecture. The quantum component consists of a parameterized quantum circuit that encodes atmospheric state information and produces parameterization outputs. The classical component handles data preprocessing, feature engineering, and post-processing of quantum circuit outputs. The quantum circuit design follows a layered architecture with alternating entangling layers and single-qubit rotation layers.

## Results

The framework demonstrates several key capabilities that address challenges in climate modeling. First, the quantum neural network parameterization provides a path toward quantum advantages in climate modeling, building on demonstrated success in cloud cover prediction (Schwabe et al., 2025). The layered quantum circuit architecture enables efficient representation of complex atmospheric relationships while maintaining trainability on current quantum hardware.

Second, the multi-agent coordination architecture provides transparency and robustness through log-mediated communication. Following the Dealog framework, our implementation includes specialized agents for StateAgent, RadiationAgent, ConvectionAgent, CloudAgent, OceanAgent, VerificationAgent, and SynthesisAgent. This specialization enables modular development and testing of individual components.

Third, the evolutionary trust mechanism ensures quality control without centralized authority. By adapting the Ev-Trust approach for scientific collaboration, we create incentives for accurate parameterization and rigorous verification. The replicator dynamics ensure that trustworthy agents receive higher reputation, creating evolutionary pressure toward high-quality contributions.

## Discussion

Our framework has significant implications for democratizing access to climate modeling capabilities. The decentralized architecture enables participation from institutions with varying computational resources, potentially allowing researchers in the Global South to contribute to and benefit from advanced climate modeling. The log-based coordination approach supports knowledge co-production, enabling transparent discussion of modeling decisions.

However, several challenges remain. Quantum hardware limitations require careful circuit design to maintain performance despite noise and errors. Scalability must be addressed through parallel computation and efficient communication protocols. Validation and uncertainty quantification require further development to ensure reliability for scientific and policy applications.

The framework also raises important questions about the future of scientific collaboration. Decentralized approaches to climate modeling may help address global inequities in climate science, but realizing this potential requires investments in computational infrastructure and capacity building, particularly in currently underserved regions.
