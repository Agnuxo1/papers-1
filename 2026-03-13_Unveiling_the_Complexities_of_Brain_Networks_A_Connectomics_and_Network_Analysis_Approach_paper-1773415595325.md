## Abstract

The human brain is a complex, intricate network of interconnected neurons, which form the foundation of cognitive and behavioral processes. However, the intricate details of brain connectivity and network organization remain poorly understood. This study employed a connectomics and network analysis approach to investigate the structural and functional properties of brain networks. Utilizing diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) and resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging (rs-fMRI) data, we constructed brain networks for 100 healthy individuals and analyzed their topological properties using graph theory and machine learning techniques. Our results revealed a high degree of modularity, small-worldness, and centrality in brain networks, with hubs located within the default mode network (DMN) and the salience network (SN). These findings provide novel insights into the organization and function of brain networks and have implications for our understanding of neurological and psychiatric disorders.

## Introduction

The human brain is a complex, dynamic system consisting of approximately 86 billion neurons, each forming an average of 7,000 synapses (Koch, 2012). The intricate connectivity between neurons gives rise to complex network properties, including modularity, small-worldness, and centrality (Sporns et al., 2005). Recent advances in neuroimaging techniques, such as diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) and resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging (rs-fMRI), have facilitated the development of connectomics and network analysis approaches to investigate brain connectivity and network organization (Buckner et al., 2013).

This study aimed to contribute to the field of connectomics and network analysis in three concrete ways:

1.  **Investigating the structural and functional properties of brain networks**: By combining DTI and rs-fMRI data, we sought to gain a deeper understanding of the complex relationships between brain structure and function.
2.  **Analyzing the topological properties of brain networks**: We employed graph theory and machine learning techniques to investigate the modularity, small-worldness, and centrality of brain networks.
3.  **Identifying hubs and modules in brain networks**: Our study aimed to reveal the location and characteristics of hubs and modules within brain networks, which are critical for information processing and integration.

## Methodology

This study employed a connectomics and network analysis approach to investigate the structural and functional properties of brain networks. We used DTI data to construct structural brain networks and rs-fMRI data to construct functional brain networks. The DTI data were acquired using a 3T MRI scanner (Philips Medical Systems) with a diffusion gradient scheme of 30 directions and a b-value of 1,000 s/mm^2. The rs-fMRI data were acquired using a 3T MRI scanner (Philips Medical Systems) with a TR of 2,000 ms and a TE of 30 ms.

We constructed brain networks using the following steps:

1.  **Image processing**: We used the FSL (FMRIB Software Library) toolkit to perform image processing, including motion correction, brain extraction, and registration to a standard space.
2.  **Network construction**: We used the Brain Connectivity Toolbox (BCT) to construct brain networks from the DTI and rs-fMRI data.
3.  **Graph analysis**: We used the BCT to perform graph analysis, including the calculation of modularity, small-worldness, and centrality measures.

## Results

Our results revealed a high degree of modularity, small-worldness, and centrality in brain networks. The modularity measure, calculated using the Louvain algorithm (Blondel et al., 2008), was found to be significantly higher in brain networks than in random networks (p < 0.001). The small-worldness measure, calculated using the SW index (Watts & Strogatz, 1998), was found to be significantly higher in brain networks than in random networks (p < 0.001). The centrality measure, calculated using the Degree Centrality metric (Freeman, 1977), was found to be significantly higher in brain networks than in random networks (p < 0.001).

We identified hubs and modules within brain networks using the following steps:

1.  **Node degree calculation**: We calculated the node degree for each brain network using the Degree Centrality metric (Freeman, 1977).
2.  **Hub identification**: We identified hubs as nodes with a degree greater than the 95th percentile of the degree distribution.
3.  **Module identification**: We identified modules as sub-networks with a high modularity measure and a low number of inter-module edges.

Our results revealed that hubs were located within the default mode network (DMN) and the salience network (SN). The DMN was found to be highly connected, with hubs located within the medial prefrontal cortex (MPFC) and the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC). The SN was found to be highly connected, with hubs located within the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and the insula.

## Discussion

Our study provides novel insights into the organization and function of brain networks. The high degree of modularity, small-worldness, and centrality in brain networks suggests that brain networks are highly optimized for information processing and integration. The identification of hubs and modules within brain networks suggests that brain networks are highly specialized for specific functions, such as attention and emotion regulation.

Our findings have implications for our understanding of neurological and psychiatric disorders. Abnormalities in brain connectivity and network organization have been implicated in a range of neurological and psychiatric disorders, including Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and major depressive disorder. Our study provides a framework for the investigation of brain connectivity and network organization in these disorders, which may lead to the development of novel treatments and interventions.
