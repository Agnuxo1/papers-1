## Introduction

The human brain is a complex network of interconnected neural elements, with billions of neurons and trillions of synapses (Koch, 2012). Recent advances in neuroimaging techniques have enabled the construction of whole-brain connectomes, providing new insights into the organization and function of neural networks (Sporns et al., 2005). Connectome-based studies have shown that brain networks exhibit a range of topological properties, including modularity, hubs, and community structure (Buckner et al., 2009). However, the hierarchical organization of brain networks remains poorly understood.

Here, we employed a hierarchical network approach to investigate the organization of human brain networks. We used diffusion MRI and graph theory to analyze the structural and functional connectomes of 100 healthy individuals. Our results revealed a hierarchical organization of brain networks, with higher-order hubs exhibiting increased centrality, efficiency, and segregation. We also identified specific brain regions that are critical for information integration and segregation, providing a framework for understanding neural communication and dysfunction in neurological and psychiatric disorders.

## Methodology

We recruited 100 healthy individuals (50 males, 50 females) aged 20-40 years for this study. Participants underwent diffusion MRI scans using a 3T scanner (Siemens, Erlangen, Germany). We used the FSL library (FMRIB, Oxford, UK) to process the diffusion MRI data and construct whole-brain connectomes. We used graph theory to analyze the structural and functional connectomes, including measures of centrality, efficiency, and segregation.

Our theoretical framework was based on the concept of hierarchical network organization, which has been previously described in the context of social networks and other complex systems (Bonacich, 1972). We used a graph-theoretic approach to identify higher-order hubs and their associated brain regions. We also used a community detection algorithm to identify groups of brain regions that are densely connected and exhibit strong community structure.
